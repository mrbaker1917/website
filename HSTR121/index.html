COURSE DESCRIPTION: This course is supposed to explore "central issues in post-1880 U.S. history by examining the interplay between political culture and popular music. Major themes include the rise and fall of Jim Crow segregation, technological change, Cold War culture, the Vietnam War and the protest movements, changing gender relations and the debate over globalization." I plan to focus the course more on contemporary events, and more on the origins and development of one of the most popular forms of modern musical expression: Rock and Roll. Topics include the origins of the genre in the southern United States, coming out of a mix of jazz, blues, country, and bluegrass music; the commercialization of rock music; the transformation of African-American music for white audiences; the British Invasions; transnational 1960s youth culture; the creation of mass music consumption; the means and the message (radio, MTV, napster, bittorrent, and iTunes); the internationalization of Rock music; the splintering of Rock and other related popular forms across the globe. Emphasis will be placed on the dual processes of Rock's strong tendency to question the status quo and capitalism's remarkable ability to co-opt this questioning.

Expectations and Goals:

  • To provide students with a basic understanding of the causes of the rise of Rock and Roll as a music genre
  • To guide students through the development of the genre into a social, cultural, and economic project
  • To encourage students to think more critically about the origins of the globalized music industry
  • By getting students involved in the editing of Wikipedia rock related pages, to help them to understand how (mis-)information is created and spread
  • To improve students' writing and critical thinking about the ways film portrays rock and roll and its musicians.

Course Materials:

  • Szatmary, David P. Rockin' in Time. Boston: Pearson, 2014.
  • Marcus, Greil. History of Rock 'n' Roll in Ten Songs. New Haven: Yale University Press, 2015.
  • One article each week from Rolling Stone Magazine's freely available "Coverwall" archive [link].
  • Various other short articles from various journals, as well as videos and other media.

Class Procedure:

Class periods will include a mixture of discussion, student presentations, and lectures. Because considerable class time will be spent in discussion, it is absolutely crucial that students do the reading, listening, and viewing of class materials before each class, and come prepared to talk about these materials in a critical and constructive way. In addition, attendance at class is very important, in order that we can carry on a discussion across multiple class periods. Please strive to keep up with the course materials and come to class on time.
